How Long Does a Website Take to Build?

Realistic timelines from discovery to launch. What happens at each phase. Why rushing websites doesn't work.

← Home Cost Guide Static Sites

The Quick Answer

  • Small brochure site (5–10 pages): 4–6 weeks
  • Medium business site (15–20 pages): 6–8 weeks
  • WordPress migration: 2–4 weeks
  • Complex site with integrations: 8–12 weeks

Timeline assumes clear scope, timely feedback, and no scope creep.

Why It Takes This Long (It's Not Wasted Time)

Building a quality website isn't just coding. Here's what's actually happening:

  • Discovery (1–2 weeks): Understanding your business, audience, competitive landscape, and strategy
  • Design (1–2 weeks): Multiple rounds of mockups, feedback, iteration
  • Development (2–3 weeks): Hand-coding every page, custom interactions, testing
  • Testing & Launch (1 week): Browser testing, mobile testing, performance testing, DNS setup

Skip any of these and the quality suffers. A site built in 2 weeks is either a template (which we don't use) or missing critical work.

The Full Timeline Breakdown

Phase 1
1–2 weeks

Discovery & Strategy

What happens: We interview you about your business, audience, goals, and constraints. We research your competition and define the site strategy.

Your input: 1–2 hours for interviews, feedback on competitive analysis, answers to strategic questions.

Deliverables: Strategy document, messaging framework, site architecture, technical requirements.

Phase 2
1–2 weeks

Design & Mockups

What happens: High-fidelity design mockups for key pages. We design desktop and mobile layouts, typography, color application, interactive elements.

Your input: Feedback on visual direction, messaging, layout. Up to 3 rounds of revision included.

Deliverables: Approved design mockups, design system, component library for consistency.

Phase 3
2–3 weeks

Development & Build

What happens: Hand-coded HTML, CSS, and JavaScript implementation. Progressive builds so you can see the work weekly.

Your input: Review of development previews, content approval, feedback on functionality.

Deliverables: Fully functional site with all pages, forms, animations, integrations.

Phase 4
1 week

Testing, Optimisation & Launch

What happens: Browser testing (Chrome, Firefox, Safari), mobile testing, performance optimisation, security checks, Lighthouse audit, DNS setup, deployment.

Your input: Final approval, domain nameserver update (if needed).

Deliverables: Live website, source code, documentation, training on how to edit the site.

What Affects Timeline?

Things That Speed It Up ✓

  • Clear scope upfront: Knowing exactly what you want saves revision time
  • You provide content: We don't have to write or source it
  • Quick feedback: Slow approvals delay every phase
  • Simple design: Fewer custom interactions = faster development
  • Minimal integrations: Each API integration adds 1–2 weeks
  • You're decisive: Clear direction on messaging, design, features

Things That Slow It Down ✗

  • Unclear scope: Scope creep (adding features mid-project) adds weeks
  • You don't have content: Waiting for copy, images, or product information delays launch
  • Slow approval cycles: Waiting days for feedback on each phase
  • Complex integrations: Payment processing, inventory systems, APIs need testing
  • Multiple decision-makers: Committee review adds 1–2 weeks per round
  • Custom features: Anything unique takes longer to build and test
  • Last-minute changes: Redesigns mid-project reset the timeline

Can We Go Faster?

Rush Delivery (< 2 weeks)

Technically possible for very small projects (5–7 pages, simple design), but:

  • Quality suffers: Less testing, less refinement, less polish
  • Cost increases: Rush fees add 20–50% to project cost
  • Scope must be tiny: No room for changes, iterations, or unexpected issues

Our recommendation: Plan for 4–8 weeks. You get a better site, better testing, and don't sacrifice quality for speed.

Realistic Expectations

  • Week 1–2: Discovery phase. You're answering questions, we're learning your business.
  • Week 3–4: Design phase. You see mockups, give feedback, we iterate.
  • Week 5–6: Development. The site comes together, you see progress weekly.
  • Week 7: Testing, optimisation, launch preparation.
  • Week 8: Live. Site launches. You get training and 30 days of free support.

This assumes timely feedback and no major scope changes.

Common Timeline Questions

Can you build a website in 2 weeks?

Only for very small projects (5 pages, simple design, no integrations). Anything larger loses quality. We'd recommend planning for 4+ weeks to get a site you're proud of.

What if I'm in a hurry?

Tell us upfront. For projects with tight timelines, we can compress phases, but it affects cost (rush fees) and potentially quality. We'll be honest about what's realistic.

Why does discovery take 1–2 weeks?

It's worth it. Understanding your business strategy, audience, and goals results in a better site. A site built without strategy is just a pretty template. We need 1–2 weeks to do it right.

Can I see the website earlier than launch?

Yes. During development (week 5–6), we share progress updates every few days. You can see the site coming together and give feedback as we build.

What if feedback is delayed? Does that extend the timeline?

Yes. If you approve mockups on week 4 instead of week 3, we don't start development until week 4. We can't proceed without your sign-off. Fast feedback = faster launch.

What about revisions after launch?

30 days of free support are included for bug fixes and minor tweaks. After that, changes are billed at $60/hour or included in the optional $300/month support package.

Why not just use a template to launch faster?

Templates are fast but generic. Your site looks like everyone else's. Custom-built sites take longer but are unique, better aligned with your brand, and ultimately perform better. The extra time is worth it.
